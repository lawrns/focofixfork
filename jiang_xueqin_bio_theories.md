## 1. BIOGRAPHICAL INFORMATION

### Personal Background
- **Name**: Jiang Xueqin (Chinese: 江学勤; pinyin: Jiāng Xuéqín)
- **Born**: 1976 (some sources say 1974)
- **Birthplace**: Guangdong, China
- **Citizenship**: Canadian (family emigrated from China when he was young)
- **Current Residence**: Beijing, China

### Education
- **Yale College (1999)**: Graduated with distinction, Bachelor of Arts in English Literature
- Jiang has reflected that his Yale education imparted analytical skills and life wisdom that influenced his approach to teaching and intellectual pursuits
- He has spoken about feeling like "a complete fraud" at Yale despite his academic success, which shaped his views on the conflict between external achievement and being a thoughtful individual

### Career History

#### Early Career (Pre-2008)
- Worked as a freelance journalist and documentary filmmaker
- Held positions with the United Nations in international development
- **2002**: Arrested in China for allegedly spying while investigating workers' strikes in Daqing, subsequently deported
- Taught briefly at the Affiliated High School of Peking University (Beida Fuzhong) in the late 1990s as a foreign teacher

#### Shenzhen Middle School (2008-2010)
- **Position**: Deputy Principal / Curriculum Director
- Built and managed a study abroad program to promote creativity, collaboration, and critical thinking skills
- Partnered with Principal Wang Zheng to launch experimental education reforms
- Key innovations included:
  - Student-operated coffee shop to teach entrepreneurship and empathy
  - English-language lending library with thousands of books
  - School newspaper
  - Elimination of mandatory military training (replaced with hiking or yoga)
  - Nine Ivy-educated foreign teachers
  - Separate cafeteria with professionally nutrition-calibrated meals
  - Optional uniforms, allowed hair dye and dating

#### Peking University High School (2010-2012)
- **Position**: Program Director, International Division
- Built and managed the International Division to promote global citizenship and service learning
- Continued similar reform measures as at Shenzhen
- **2012**: Contract terminated via email while on vacation in Canada

#### Tsinghua University High School (2013-2014)
- **Position**: Deputy Principal
- Continued education reform work

#### Post-2014 Activities
- Currently advises schools across China on curriculum design emphasizing creativity, critical thinking, and global perspectives
- Based in Chengdu (since 2018) for lower cost of living
- Works at an international school in Beijing (as of 2022)
- Runs the YouTube channel "Predictive History" (launched around 2023)

### Current Affiliations
- **Researcher**: Global Education Innovation Initiative, Harvard Graduate School of Education
- **Fellow**: Royal Society of Arts (RSA)
- **Judge**: Global Teacher Prize, Global Student Prize, World's Best School Prizes
- **Columnist**: New York Times Chinese website, China Youth Daily

### Personal Life
- Married with at least one child (son, as of 2022)
- Lives in a rented 118-square-meter apartment in Chengdu (2,800 yuan/month)
- Has explored interests outside education including cooking, boxing, writing, and stand-up comedy

## 2. PUBLISHED WORKS

### Books

#### "Creative China" (创新中国教育, 2014)
- **Publisher**: Central Compilation & Translation Press
- **ISBN**: 978-7-5117-2072-6
- **Language**: Chinese

**Summary of Content, Themes, and Key Arguments:**

The book documents Jiang's education reform initiatives in Chinese public schools, particularly his work at Shenzhen Middle School and Peking University High School.

**Core Themes:**

1. **Critique of the Gaokao System**
   - The gaokao was effective for producing engineers and mid-level bureaucrats for China's manufacturing-based economy
   - It filters out people with the highest analytical intelligence
   - Perfect for China's 1980s-1990s sweatshop economy focused on organizing masses and producing goods for export
   - However, it stifles creativity and entrepreneurial spirit needed for a knowledge-based society

2. **The Need for Educational Transformation**
   - China needs to transition from manufacturing-based to knowledge-based economy
   - Requires entrepreneurs, designers, managers—people China doesn't have in sufficient numbers
   - The education system must evolve to foster creativity, critical thinking, and independent thinking

3. **Practical Reform Initiatives**
   - Setting up lending libraries instead of test prep
   - Creating school newspapers and student-operated businesses (coffee shop)
   - Emphasizing reading, writing, and real-world work experience
   - Teaching empathy through service roles (waiter in coffee shop)

4. **The Coffee Shop Experiment**
   - Designed to teach students the complexities of entrepreneurship
   - Learning empathy and service by thinking about customer needs
   - Challenged Chinese prejudice against manual labor ("You're not supposed to use your hands because you're an intellectual")
   - Rich Chinese kids initially wanted to hire laborers rather than work themselves

**Key Quotes from the Book/Related Interviews:**
- "The gaokao was a good way to produce engineers and mid-level bureaucrats. It was perfect for these goals because it filters out people with the highest analytical intelligence."
- "We wanted to broaden children's education so they're not just test-taking machines, be it for the gaokao or the SAT."
- "I wanted to make them thoughtful individuals who would appreciate their education and be less focused on tests."
- "In the gaokao system, there was no room for reform — studying overseas is the future of Chinese education."

- **Chinese Title**: 看不见的力量 ("The Invisible Power")
- **Published**: June 2021

**Summary of Content, Themes, and Key Arguments:**

This book explores moral education, the science of success, learning, and creativity.

**Core Themes:**

1. **Moral Education**
   - Focuses on the "invisible" forces that shape education and human development
   - Explores the connection between empathy, morality, and learning

2. **The Science of Success**
   - Examines what truly leads to success beyond test scores and credentials
   - Challenges conventional metrics of achievement

3. **Learning and Creativity**
   - Builds on ideas from "Creative China"
   - Further develops frameworks for teaching creativity in constrained educational environments

4. **Reflections on Reform Failures**
   - Contains Jiang's reflections on why his reform experiments ultimately failed
   - His identification with Michael Sandel's theory that "merit is tyranny"
   - Recognition that even public schools serve to select elites

## 3. THEORIES AND FRAMEWORKS

### Educational Philosophy

#### Core Beliefs

1. **Education Should Foster Creativity and Critical Thinking**
   - The best American institutions have "a culture and tradition of openness, diversity, and risk-taking that China must emulate if China is to progress as a society and as a culture"
   - Education should not be about test scores but about fostering thoughtful individuals

2. **Empathy as Foundation**
   - "Empathy is like water... It's fundamental to life and society cannot function without it. Empathy nourishes us and it's fundamental to who we are."
   - Empathy is the foundation for collaboration, communication, and the ability to understand and work with diverse groups
   - "From an intellectual perspective, the ability to articulate and read another person's mind is the most intellectual ability."

3. **Critique of Achievement-Focused Systems**
   - "In general, any education system that highlights achievement and goals above process and attitude is, in my opinion, bad for students."
   - Systems that reward utilitarian, unethical, shortsighted behavior destroy students' intrinsic curiosity, creativity, and love of learning

4. **Global Citizenship**
   - "We really need to educate a new generation of Chinese students who are able to have empathy and who are able to understand and respect other cultures."
   - In an interconnected world, students need to understand and respect other cultures for a peaceful and prosperous future

### Critique of Chinese Education System (Gaokao)

#### Historical Context
- China's Cultural Revolution used education as a steamroller to level class divisions
- After Deng Xiaoping, education became a tool for economic modernization
- Based on Soviet Russia's Leninist-Stalinist system with antecedents in China's keju system

#### Achievements of the System
1. Brought basic literacy and numeracy to most of the student population
2. Created a young literate workforce that could transition from fields to factories
3. Filtered students with highest analytical intelligence to produce engineers and technocrats

#### Current Problems
- "Chinese students are focused and disciplined learners who see pain and hard work as fundamental to self-improvement"
- Students are "not equipped to display critical thinking skills or to work independently"
- "Trained to work very well in factories or bureaucracies, but they tend not to thrive in multi-cultural environments engaged in open-ended projects"
- "A fear of authority" - taught to follow orders and answer questions, not give instructions and ask questions
- "Chinese schools are built entirely around extrinsic motivation... your test score determines praise from your teachers... it determines your sense of self-worth"
- "What matters in Chinese education is... not how well you do on a test, what matters is how you do relative to your peers"

#### The "Prisoner's Dilemma"
- Parents are so afraid of their child being left behind that the arms race of high-stakes testing continues to escalate
- Parents complain about testing robbing children of childhood while enrolling four-year-olds in cram schools

### Critique of Western/American Education

- "It seems nowadays that American students put instant self-gratification and high self-esteem above all else"
- The American and Chinese education systems are "converging in their flaws"
- Both creating hyper-competitive environments that serve the elite while failing the broader population

### Framework for Teaching Creativity

#### Three Pillars (from Ashoka Proposal)

1. **Reading Literature**
   - "Literature was used to teach empathy in schools because literature is about seeing the world through someone's perspectives"
   - Writers are people with tremendous empathy for themselves and their community
   - Reading is fundamental but increasingly irrelevant in schools

2. **Group Dynamics**
   - Prevails in every class
   - Students learn to work together
   - Emphasizes self-understanding, appreciation of others' strengths and weaknesses
   - Recognizing that different people bring different things to the table

3. **Evidence and Logic (via Debate)**
   - Offering specific feedback during discussions
   - Ability to hold yourself back and offer specific feedback requires empathy
   - Prevents discussions from degenerating into insults

#### Implementation Principles
- Move away from tests to papers, presentations, and experiments
- Constantly reinforce that doing well on tests doesn't mean you're smart
- "Being smart includes the ability to work with different people and to be able to recognize the strengths of other people"
- Service learning (e.g., sending students to Botswana to work with AIDS and disabled children)

### "Predictive History" Framework

#### Overview
- YouTube channel launched around 2023
- Applies structural historical analysis, game theory, and concepts inspired by Isaac Asimov's fictional "psychohistory"
- Over 1.5 million subscribers

#### Main Series

1. **Civilization** (60+ lectures)
   - Traces narrative and ideological evolution of human societies from prehistory to present
   - Emphasizes unifying and mobilizing power of transcendent religious and civilizational belief systems
   - Draws on classical Western narrative traditions: Iliad, Aeschylus, Euripides, Alexander the Great, Virgil's Aeneid, Dante's Divine Comedy, Jewish messianic thought, Christian eschatology

2. **Geo-Strategy**
   - Applies historical analogies to current great-power politics
   - Famous Episode 8: "The Iran Trap" (May 28-29, 2024)

#### Methodology
- Uses recurring historical patterns, game theory, and long-term pattern recognition
- Not mystical but structural - "reading incentives" rather than "reading tea leaves"
- Combines historical cycles, geopolitical interests, and game-theory frameworks

#### The Three Predictions (May 2024 Lecture)
1. Donald Trump would return to the White House
2. A renewed Trump presidency would lead to military conflict with Iran
3. The US would lose this war, forever changing the global order

#### Key Geopolitical Concepts
- "To win a war, you need to avoid encirclement, mass your forces, and protect your supply lines"
- Compared potential US invasion of Iran to Athens' failed Sicilian expedition during the Peloponnesian War
- "They look like soldiers, but they are really hostages" (on US troops potentially bogged down in Iran)

### Views on Meritocracy

- Identifies with Michael Sandel's theory that "merit is tyranny"
- "Even public schools, at the end of the day, serve to select elites"
- "The gap between the have's and the have-not's in China has only widened"
- "One of the greatest lies in our lives is that the meritocracy promotes the best leaders"

### Views on Consumerism

#### "Consumerism is the Perfection of Slavery"

This is one of Jiang's most viral concepts:

- Consumerism has replaced the worker as the organizing principle of society
- This shift began in the 1980s
- Led to increased inequality and prioritization of consumerism over worker rights

**Key Arguments:**
- Traditional slavery required overseers, whips, and constant threat of violence - expensive, inefficient, generated resistance
- Modern consumerism creates a system of control so complete that victims volunteer for longer hours, deeper debt, and greater isolation while believing they are exercising freedom
- "The slave now loves his chains, polishes them, and begs for tighter ones"
- The system runs on desire rather than coercion
- "You police yourself. You compare yourself. You borrow at compound interest to finance a lifestyle that exists primarily to be photographed and envied"
- The whip has been internalized; the overseer now lives inside your skull
- Workers organize; consumers compete
- Workers go on strike; consumers refresh their feeds to see who bought a bigger house
- "The genius of the system is that it runs on desire rather than coercion"

### Views on Money and Religion

- "Money is God, God is money. That makes sense to people."
- "How do I know I have faith in God? Because I have a lot of money."
- "The Chinese dream is work hard, get into a good school, get rich, move your money and your children to the United States. If that's a dream, then eventually the civilization won't last very long."
- "The appeal of money and materialism is dying. People are just exhausted, disillusioned, and frustrated. We are moving to an age of religious revival."

### Personal Philosophy

- Self-awareness is crucial for personal growth
- True leadership is about empowering others
- There is a conflict between achieving external success and being a thoughtful individual
- Happiness and fulfillment come from more than career success
- "I believe everyone's capable of reason... therefore you have a responsibility to reason for yourself."
- "People don't want to think, people want to believe, people want to obey."
- "People aren't attracted to logic or reason, they're attracted to confidence."
- "Perception is more important than reality."
- "History doesn't care about our feelings."

## 4. KEY CONCEPTS AND TERMINOLOGY

### Education Terms

| Term | Jiang's Definition/Usage |
|------|-------------------------|
| **Empathy** | "The ability to be able to adapt the viewpoints of others... the ability to sympathize or understand what they think and to articulate their viewpoint"; "like water... fundamental to life" |
| **Creativity** | Linked to empathy; "the constant negotiation of knowing who you are and what the world demands that will lead to new ideas" |
| **Critical Thinking** | Ability to work independently, ask questions, give instructions; not just answering questions |
| **Global Citizenship** | Understanding and respecting other cultures; ability to work in multi-cultural environments |
| **Gaokao** | China's national college entrance exam; "good way to produce engineers and mid-level bureaucrats" but stifles creativity |
| **Extrinsic Motivation** | China's system built entirely around this; test scores determine praise and self-worth |
| **Intrinsic Motivation** | "Motivation 3.0" - learning for the sake of learning; what China lacks |
| **Test-taking Machines** | What both gaokao and SAT preparation turns students into |
| **Service Learning** | Learning through service to others (e.g., working with AIDS children in Botswana) |

### Predictive History Terms

| Term | Jiang's Definition/Usage |
|------|-------------------------|
| **Predictive History** | Framework using historical patterns, game theory, and pattern recognition to forecast geopolitical events |
| **Psychohistory** | Isaac Asimov's fictional concept; mathematical science of mass human behavior |
| **Game Theory** | Analyzing incentives of key players to predict behavior |
| **Historical Patterns** | Recurring structures in history that can inform future predictions |
| **Operation Iranian Freedom** | Jiang's term for predicted joint US-Israel-Saudi invasion of Iran |
| **The Iran Trap** | Lecture series applying predictive history to US-Iran conflict |

| Term | Jiang's Definition/Usage |
